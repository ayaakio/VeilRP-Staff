1.0 Discord Guide

1.1 Purpose of Discord Moderation

Discord moderation is one of the main ways staff maintain the community outside of the server itself.

Staff are expected to remain aware of what is happening in Discord and help keep the server calm, respectful, and organised.

  • Monitor active chats regularly.
  • Step in early if conversations begin to go in the wrong direction.
  • Maintain a professional staff presence at all times.
  • Help create a positive environment for the wider community.

1.2 Keeping Up to Date

Staff should not treat Discord moderation as something that only happens when they are tagged.

It is important to stay up to date with what is being said across the server so that issues can be identified before they become larger problems.

  • Check active channels consistently.
  • Be aware of repeat issues, ongoing disputes, or players causing disruption.
  • Make sure no important conversations are being ignored.
  • Pay attention to tone as well as content.

1.3 What Staff Should Look Out For

Staff should watch for anything that may negatively affect the atmosphere of the Discord.

  • Arguments between players.
  • Toxic behaviour or targeted hostility.
  • Rule-breaking conversations.
  • Spam, provocation, or repeated disruption.
  • Players attempting to stir drama or bait responses.
  • Anything that begins to create tension in the community.

2.0 Discord Moderation

2.1 General Expectations

Staff are expected to actively moderate Discord rather than only reacting after situations become serious.

  • Keep chats peaceful and within server expectations.
  • Address problems before they escalate.
  • Be visible and approachable without being confrontational.
  • Use moderation tools fairly and consistently.

2.2 Handling Arguments

When arguments begin, the first goal is to de-escalate the situation.

  • Step in early and tell users to calm the conversation down.
  • Keep your response neutral and professional.
  • Do not take sides publicly.
  • Redirect the conversation where possible.
  • If needed, tell users to move on or stop replying further.

If users continue to argue after staff intervention, stronger moderation action may be required.

2.3 Using Timeouts

Timeouts are one of the main tools used to stop arguments and disruptive behaviour from continuing.

  • Use timeouts when a user continues to argue, stir drama, or disrupt chat after being told to stop.
  • Choose a timeout length that fits the severity of the situation.
  • Timeouts should be used to cool situations down, not as an emotional response.
  • Be consistent with timeout usage across different players and situations.

A timeout is often the best option when a player needs to be removed from the conversation temporarily without escalating straight to a heavier punishment.

2.4 How to Open the Moderator Panel

Discord’s moderation tools are built directly into the user menu.

  • Right-click the user you want to moderate.
  • This opens the user moderation menu.
  • From there, you can view options such as timeout and other moderation actions available to your role.
  • Check the user profile if you need to confirm their roles, permissions, or account details before acting.

Always make sure you are selecting the correct user before taking action.

2.5 Moderator Tools

Discord provides several built-in moderation tools that staff should be familiar with.

  • Viewing user profiles.
  • Checking roles and permissions.
  • Applying timeouts.
  • Reviewing previous behaviour through message history where needed.

Use these tools to gather context before acting, not just to react quickly without understanding what happened.

2.6 Staff Behaviour While Moderating

How staff speak in Discord matters just as much as the action they take.

  • Remain calm and professional.
  • Do not argue with users.
  • Do not respond emotionally, sarcastically, or aggressively.
  • Be clear and direct without being hostile.
  • Lead by example in every public channel.

3.0 Good Practice

3.1 Best Practice for Discord

  • Be proactive rather than reactive.
  • Read the full context before stepping in.
  • Use the lightest action needed to resolve the issue.
  • Stay consistent in how rules are applied.
  • Escalate serious or unclear situations if needed.
  • Keep moderation fair, calm, and justifiable.

3.2 When to Escalate

Not every issue needs to be handled alone.

  • Escalate situations if they involve serious harassment, threats, or repeated behaviour.
  • Escalate if you are personally involved or feel conflicted.
  • Escalate if you are unsure which action is appropriate.
  • Escalate if a punishment may need to go beyond a timeout or simple moderation action.

4.0 What Not To Do

4.1 Poor Moderation Practice

  • Do not ignore obvious problems in chat.
  • Do not let arguments continue unchecked.
  • Do not use moderation tools out of frustration.
  • Do not publicly argue with members.
  • Do not take action without understanding the situation first.
  • Do not favour friends or treat users inconsistently.
